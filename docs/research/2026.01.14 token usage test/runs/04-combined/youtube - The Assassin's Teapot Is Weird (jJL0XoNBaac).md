## Assassin's Teapot Mechanics

**TL;DR**: Teapot with two chambers dispenses different liquids by covering air holes that control flow via atmospheric pressure and surface tension.

### How It Works

**What**: Two internal chambers, each connected to spout and separate air hole on handle.

**Why**: Covering a hole blocks air entry to that chamber, preventing liquid flow.

**How**: Cover hole A → chamber B pours. Cover hole B → chamber A pours. Cover neither → both mix. Cover both → no flow.

**What Then**: Serve different liquids by controlling which holes you cover.

### Physics Principle

**What**: Surface tension prevents air bubbles sneaking past liquid through narrow spout.

**Why**: Atmospheric pressure holds liquid in sealed chambers without air entry path.

**How**: Air bubbling through spout increases water surface area, which surface tension opposes. Only path is through top holes.

## Comment Insights

**Key Takeaway**: Practical applications range from serving multiple beverages simultaneously to party tricks, with important safety considerations about chamber orientation and cross-contamination.

### Historical Context
- **Chinese Origins**: Called "鸳鸯转香壶", first appeared in Han Dynasty (241 BC). Used by Queen Lü Zhi to poison emperor's mother, later by Emperor Zhao Guangyi to kill Emperor Li Yu in Northern Song Dynasty (@yiy149)

### Safety Considerations
- **Pour Order Matters**: Always pour your own (safe) drink first to avoid poison residue on spout (@disturbedNeutrino)
- **Chamber Orientation**: Top/bottom design causes cross-contamination when pouring - top liquid drips into bottom chamber. Side-by-side design safer (@Thedarkbunnyrabbit, @Krispymelon, @stickmanonastick6089)
- **Poison Placement**: Put poison in bottom chamber to minimize cross-contamination risk (@endorkugo, @Krispymelon)
