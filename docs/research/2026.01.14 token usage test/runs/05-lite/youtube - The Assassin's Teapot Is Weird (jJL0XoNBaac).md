## Summary

**TL;DR**: Assassin's teapot uses two chambers with top holes to selectively pour different liquids - covering holes traps liquid via atmospheric pressure and surface tension.

## How the Assassin's Teapot Works

**What**: A teapot with two internal chambers, each with a hole at the top and both leading to the spout - covering different holes selects which chamber pours.

**Why**: Demonstrates atmospheric pressure and surface tension - when a hole is covered, air cannot enter the chamber, so pressure differential prevents liquid from flowing out.

**How**:
- Blue drink: cover hole A
- Yellow drink: cover hole B
- Green drink (mix): cover neither hole
- No drink: cover both holes
- Mechanism: Air must replace exiting liquid. Covered hole blocks air entry from top. Air cannot enter through spout because surface tension prevents bubbling (bubbles would increase surface area, energetically unfavorable).

**What Then**: Same principle applies to bottle-with-gauze trick, straw suction (atmosphere pushes liquid up, not lungs pulling), and magic "inexhaustible bottle" tricks with multiple chambers.

## Hidden Gems

**Useful vs Pedantic Physics Language**: Acceptable to use "suction force" or "cold flows" as conceptual shortcuts, provided underlying physics is understood (atmospheric pressure has limits ~100kPa, temperature has absolute zero floor). Analogous to semiconductor physics using "positively charged holes" - useful abstraction despite not being literal.

## Comment Insights

**Key Takeaway**: Historical Chinese origins and practical safety considerations for avoiding cross-contamination when using dual-chamber teapots.

**Historical Context**:
- Chinese name "鸳鸯转香壶" dates to Han Dynasty (241 BC) - Queen Lü Zhi used it to poison Emperor Liu Gong's mother; Emperor Zhao Guangyi later used it against Emperor Li Yu (@yiy149)
- Also known as "magician's tea pot" - used in stage magic and TV shows for serving different drinks from same container (@spangle, @WildWillieWiccan)
- Featured in Chinese historical novels, demonstrating extensive knowledge of ancient technology (@d_crown)

**Safety & Design Corrections**:
- Always pour yourself first - poison residue on spout can contaminate your drink (@disturbedNeutrino)
- Put poison in bottom chamber, not top - top liquid backflows into bottom during pouring (@Krispymelon, @endorkugo, @Thedarkbunnyrabbit)
- Side-by-side chamber design safer than vertical stacking for avoiding cross-contamination (@Thedarkbunnyrabbit)
- Default convention: poison on top (easier to cover), safe drink on bottom - only cover top hole when serving yourself (@davidgruen7423)

**Physics Corrections**:
- Straw drinking uses tongue to create vacuum in mouth cavity, not lungs - using lungs risks drowning (@carlz0r)
- Wouldn't work in vacuum partly because liquids vaporize at zero pressure, creating some pressure anyway (@purungo)

**Modern Applications**:
- Waiters serving regular/decaf coffee from single pot (@Sam_on_YouTube)
- Hot tea/iced tea service with insulation between chambers (@WildWillieWiccan)
- Party trick: serving tea then milk from same pot, or making guests debate drink flavors (@Rae.Harper, @il0stthegam3)
