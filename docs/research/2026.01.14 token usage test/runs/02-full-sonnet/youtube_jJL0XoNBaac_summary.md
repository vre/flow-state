## Assassin's Teapot: How It Works

**TL;DR**: The assassin's teapot uses two separate chambers and strategic finger placement over air holes to pour different liquids (or poison) using atmospheric pressure and surface tension.

### The Mechanism

**What**: A teapot with two internal chambers, each connected to the spout and to separate air holes on the handle - covering different holes selects which chamber pours.

**Why**: By controlling which hole is open, you control which chamber can equalize air pressure and thus pour - covered chambers remain sealed by atmospheric pressure.

**How**: When you cover a hole, air cannot enter that chamber through the top. For liquid to exit through the spout, air must enter to replace it, but surface tension prevents air bubbles from forming through the narrow spout opening. The atmosphere (100 kPa) pushes against the liquid, keeping it trapped. Uncovered chambers allow air in from the top, letting liquid flow freely.

**What Then**: You can pour three drinks (blue chamber, yellow chamber, or mixed) plus a "null drink" by covering both holes.

## Hidden Gems

**Useful Pedantry**: While technically atmospheric pressure pushes liquid up a straw rather than suction pulling it, it's pragmatic to use "suction" language if you understand the underlying physics and its limits (e.g., max 100 kPa). Similar to how physicists discuss "flow of positively charged holes" in semiconductors or "flow of cold" - useful abstractions that work until you hit physical boundaries.
