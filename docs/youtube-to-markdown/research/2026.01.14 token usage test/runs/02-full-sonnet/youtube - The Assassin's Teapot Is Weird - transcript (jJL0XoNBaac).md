### The Physics Behind It: Atmospheric Pressure

Instead of a gauze, there's a small hole—big enough to push a straw into. It floats to the top of the bottle. It's a really cool trick. Strictly speaking, it's not surface tension that's keeping the liquid in place—it's air pressure. The assassin's teapot and these bottle tricks wouldn't work in a vacuum. ### Surface Tension: The Key Mechanism

Tangent over. The point is, we all know that for the water to get out, air needs to be able to get in. With your finger off the hole, air can get in at the top. But if you cover the hole, the only way for the air to get in is through the spout, and that's where surface tension comes in. Surface tension acts to minimize surface area. One way for the water to get out is if the air can sneak past it on the way. But what would that look like? You'd have a drip of water starting on one side and a bubble of air forming on the other side. But see how that increases the surface area of the water? Because surface tension acts to decrease the surface area, that's not energetically favorable. So you don't get this bubbling effect, and instead the liquid is trapped inside the chamber.
