## TL;DR
The assassin's teapot can pour three different drinks from one vessel by covering different holes, working through surface tension principles that prevent air entry when holes are blocked.

## The Assassin's Teapot Mechanism

**What**: A teapot with two internal chambers, each connected to a hole on top and both leading to the spout, allowing selection of three drinks (blue, yellow, or mixed green) by covering different holes.

**Why**: When you cover a hole, you prevent air from entering that chamber. Without air entry, atmospheric pressure prevents liquid from flowing out - the same principle as covering a straw opening with your finger.

**How**: The teapot has two side-by-side chambers. Covering the blue hole gets blue drink, covering the yellow hole gets yellow drink, covering neither gets green (both chambers pour), covering both gets nothing. Surface tension at the narrow spout prevents air bubbles from sneaking past water droplets because that would increase surface area, which is energetically unfavorable.

## Hidden Gems

### Suction Force Pedantry
The video argues for accepting useful simplifications like "suction force" or "flow of cold" as long as you understand the underlying physics. While technically it's atmospheric pressure pushing liquid up a straw (not suction pulling), the concept remains useful - just remember limitations like the 100 kilopascal atmospheric pressure ceiling. Similar to how physicists acceptably discuss "flow of positively charged holes" in semiconductors despite holes not actually flowing.

### 2D Transparent Demonstration
To explain the mechanism, the creator built a transparent 2D version by arranging chambers vertically instead of side-by-side, demonstrating each drink selection with colored liquids through clear acrylic panels.
