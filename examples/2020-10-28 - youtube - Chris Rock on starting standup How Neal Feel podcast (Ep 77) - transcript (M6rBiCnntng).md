### Seeing Eddie Murphy and the Decision to Do Comedy

So you were rapping, scratching, working at Red Lobster, and then you saw Eddie at Radio City. I saw him do his show—it was like, "Okay, that's what I should be doing." The Radio City story is basically about finding my path. My father had gotten the newspaper with a full-page ad announcing Eddie Murphy performing at Radio City, with tickets going on sale the next day. I thought, "I'll get there at 11 o'clock and buy some tickets." This was back in prehistoric times when you had to physically go somewhere to buy tickets. [[00:00:00]](https://youtube.com/watch?v=M6rBiCnntng_transcript&t=0s)

#### The Six-Block Line

The line was six city blocks long—the longest line I'd ever seen in my life. It was like one of those Depression-era lines. Just brothers waiting because Eddie Murphy was going to be in a building. I knew I'd have to wait six months, but there I was. While I was standing in line reading the newspaper, I saw an ad for "Comic Strip Audition Night." I thought, "Maybe I should go there." I left the line, walked from Radio City to the Comic Strip, and found out audition night was that same night. [[00:00:44]](https://youtube.com/watch?v=M6rBiCnntng_transcript&t=44s)

### First Audition Story

About 40 people were outside waiting to pick a number. There were only seven numbers, and if you picked one of those seven, you got to perform that night. I got number seven. I'd never performed before. By the way, of those 40 people, maybe 30 had already auditioned before—it was just a regular open mic. They were like, "Your first time? Okay, get this one under your belt. You'll be good one day." [[00:01:48]](https://youtube.com/watch?v=M6rBiCnntng_transcript&t=108s)

### Early Success

I was the only person who passed that night. Butler had passed the week before me—rest in peace, I'm kidding. She's alive. She was funny. I think I did have "Here Comes the Neighborhood" that night. I made some joke about Miles Davis being so black that lightning bugs follow him into daytime. A couple more minutes of jokes, and I killed. It felt easy—no self-doubt, just thinking, "What's the big deal?" I was waiting for Eddie Murphy, and I proceeded to kill for about three weeks straight. [[00:02:36]](https://youtube.com/watch?v=M6rBiCnntng_transcript&t=156s)

#### The Incredible First Year

My first three weeks, I was annihilating. They were thinking about bringing in the SNL people to see me. My first year was incredible. But I brought a girl—my girlfriend—and I proceeded to bomb for the next three years. It took me three years to get back to that level. I think it was about confidence and arrogance. Once you cross that line from confidence into arrogance, it kills you. You have to stay confident, but that arrogance destroys everything. [[00:03:45]](https://youtube.com/watch?v=M6rBiCnntng_transcript&t=225s)

### Years of Bombing and Reflection on Being Too Young

I was probably too cocky. I was young—19, 20 years old—and I looked like I was 13. I weighed about 130 pounds. Nothing fit me. I was that picture from 1990: skin and bones, everything too big on me. Here's the thing about stand-up: there's a reason there's no Justin Bieber of comedy. Eddie Murphy is the closest we've ever had to a young comedian who succeeded, and most comedians are 30 years old when they start. One guy was 22 and absolutely killed it, but everyone else is a grown man or woman. I was young, and part of the reason it doesn't work is that nobody believes you at that age. You have no perspective, no point of view. What do you know? Nobody takes a kid seriously. But they'll take a joke about Miles Davis being black—anyone of any age gets that. So I had silly material.
