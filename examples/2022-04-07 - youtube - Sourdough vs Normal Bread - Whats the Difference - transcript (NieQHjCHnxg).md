## What is Sourdough?

When most people think of sourdough, they think of that tangy, rustic bread you can only really get at fancy artisan bakeries. But it hasn't always been that way. In fact, until about a hundred years ago, all bread was sourdough. Contrary to popular belief, the term "sourdough" doesn't have anything to do with the flavor of the bread—it has to do with the way it's made. About 10,000 years ago in ancient Egypt, people discovered that if they let a mixture of flour and water sit out for an extended period of time, it would start to bubble and rise. When they then cooked that mixture, it would result in a light, airy final product that was much tastier than the non-leavened flatbreads they were making up until that point. Whether or not they realized it, yeast was creating those bubbles. Of course, they hadn't added any yeast to the bread—it was the yeast naturally present in their flour and water. When provided with the right conditions, this yeast could grow and multiply, causing the bread to rise. The use of this naturally cultivated yeast, rather than commercial yeast like the kind you see in packets at the grocery store, is what actually defines the bread as sourdough. That's the only requirement. Sourdough bread doesn't need to taste sour, although it does tend to be sour due to the naturally occurring bacteria that forms alongside the yeast. ## The Problems with Commercial Yeast

However, that also leads to some major problems. When you add commercial yeast to your dough, it rises quickly, but since that fermentation process is so quick, it doesn't allow enough time for the wheat to break down properly. On top of that, the commercial yeast doesn't have the same biodiversity that a sourdough starter does. In other words, when you make a yeasted bread, you're literally just mixing flour, water, and yeast. Sure, it creates an edible and airy final product that is technically bread, but it'll basically just taste like cooked flour with a very slight fermented flavor from the yeast. [[00:01:59]](https://youtube.com/watch?v=NieQHjCHnxg&t=119s)

With sourdough bread, we're using a sourdough starter that has already been fermenting for weeks, months, or even years. So it not only contains a healthy culture of yeast, but it also contains lots of healthy bacteria which, when added to your dough, will work to break down the wheat, converting it into tasty, complex acids while also making the bread easier to digest and more nutritious. In simple terms, you could think of sourdough as undergoing complete breakdown during fermentation, whereas yeasted dough, while it does rise during fermentation, doesn't have a chance to fully break down. As a result, sourdough bread ends up with much more aromatic and complex flavor, with nuances really coming to the forefront—the caramelly, earthy notes of whole wheat flour or the subtle sweetness of dairy.
